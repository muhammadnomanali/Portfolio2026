Rhetoric of Risk & Harm

A Rhetorical Analysis of Nutrient-Fortified Claims in Ultra-Processed Foods.

Back to Research

A rhetorical analysis examining how stakeholders use scientific authority, strategic ambiguity, and emotive framing to construct competing narratives about ultra-processed foods.

Thesis

Through appeals to scientific authority, strategic ambiguity, and emotive framing, stakeholders construct competing narratives about UPFs, revealing how risk communication influences policy and consumer behavior. This discourse shapes individual perceptions and informs broader public health policies.

Key Findings

Scientific Authority

How claims of 'nutrient-rich' and scientific backing obscure health risks.

Strategic Ambiguity

Marketing language that conflates 'natural' with healthy.

Consumer Perception

Gap between marketed benefits and actual nutritional quality.

Policy Implications

How discourse shapes public health messaging and regulations.

Background: NOVA Classification

Ultra-processed foods (UPFs) are defined within the NOVA classification system as food products that have undergone significant industrial processing, often containing additives like preservatives, artificial colors, and flavors. A 2021 Lancet study revealed that UPFs comprise 58% of calories consumed in the U.S., coinciding with a 40% adult obesity rate.

Rhetorical Framework

Logos

Analysis of scientific claims and data presentation

Ethos

Credibility construction by industry vs. health advocates

Pathos

Emotional appeals in marketing and health warnings

Kairos

Timing and context of public health messaging

Recommendations

  • More transparent communication regarding nutritional quality
  • Critical assessment of marketing language in health guidelines
  • Consumer education on interpreting food product claims
  • Policy reform to address misleading health claims
